The Exposure Triangle

Every photo is controlled by three settings that trade off against each other.

⬡
Aperture
f/2.8 → f/8

Controls how wide the lens opens. Lower f-number = more light + shallower depth of field. f/2.8 blurs backgrounds. f/8 keeps everything sharp. On the FZ20, the range is narrow — don't expect extreme blur like a DSLR.

⏱
Shutter Speed
8s → 1/2000s

Controls how long the sensor is exposed. Faster = freezes motion. Slower = blurs motion. 1/500s+ freezes birds. 1/30s or slower creates motion blur on trains. Below 1/60s, use a surface or tripod.

◈
ISO
80 → 400

Controls sensor sensitivity to light. Higher ISO = brighter image + more grain (noise). On the FZ20's small CCD, ISO 400 is noticeably grainy. Default to ISO 80 outdoors, ISO 200 indoors.

Critical FZ20 limitation: The CCD sensor in this camera produces heavy color noise at ISO 400. Your best images will almost always be shot at ISO 80 or ISO 100. In low light, compensate by opening to f/2.8 and slowing the shutter — not by raising ISO.

Zoom & Image Compression IMPORTANT

The FZ20 has 12× optical zoom (35–420mm equivalent) and 4× digital zoom. Only use optical zoom. Digital zoom crops and degrades the image — it's the same as cropping in software afterward, but worse.

When you zoom in telephoto (toward 420mm), two things happen: (1) subjects appear magnified, and (2) the background appears compressed — buildings look stacked, distances look smaller. This is called telephoto compression and is a creative tool for urban/architecture shots.

At wide angle (35mm), the opposite happens — spaces look larger, lines converge dramatically (good for architecture). This is why your hallway shots look different depending on zoom position.

Metering — How the Camera Reads Light

The FZ20 uses evaluative/multi-zone metering by default: it samples multiple areas of the frame and averages them to set exposure. This works well for evenly lit scenes.

Problem scenario: Shooting a building against bright sky — the camera sees the bright sky and underexposes your building to compensate. Fix this with exposure compensation (the +/- button). Add +1 to +2 EV when shooting dark subjects against bright backgrounds.

You can also use spot metering on the FZ20 via the menu — this meters only the center of the frame. Useful when your subject has very different brightness from the surroundings.

Focus Modes on the FZ20

AF (Autofocus): Camera focuses automatically when you half-press shutter. The default 9-area AF works well for most subjects. For moving subjects, this may hunt (search) and miss.

MF (Manual Focus): You control focus via the thumb dial. The EVF/LCD shows a magnified preview to aid focus. Use for macro, architecture through glass, or when AF is confused by low contrast.

One-Shot AF: In MF mode, you can slide the focus switch down to get a single AF lock. This is the best of both worlds — focus where you want, then lock it.

Focus lock trick: In AF mode, half-press the shutter to lock focus on your subject, then recompose and fully press. Essential for off-center subjects.

White Balance

Light has color temperature. Daylight is blue-white (~5500K). Tungsten bulbs are orange (~3200K). Fluorescent lights are greenish. Auto White Balance (AWB) guesses and corrects this.

When AWB fails: At sunset, AWB will try to neutralize the warm tones you actually want. Set WB to "Daylight" or "Cloudy" to preserve warm golden light. For architecture under artificial lighting, AWB usually creates color casts — try the "Incandescent" or "Fluorescent" preset.

On the FZ20: MENU → White Balance → choose preset. Cloudy (approximately 6500K) adds warmth. Daylight is neutral. Incandescent removes orange casts.

Mode Dial — Every Mode Explained
What each mode does internally · When to use it · What to practice

Click any mode card to expand details.

AUTO
Full Auto
Camera controls everything.
What it does: The camera sets aperture, shutter speed, ISO, white balance, focus, and flash. You have no control over any exposure parameter.

When to use: Honestly — almost never as a learning photographer. It's fine for snapshots, but it teaches you nothing and often makes bad decisions (like firing the flash when you don't want it).

One legitimate use: Handing the camera to someone else to take a photo of you.
```
P
Program AE
Camera picks exposure, you pick everything else.
What it does: The camera automatically selects a shutter speed and aperture combination for correct exposure. You control ISO, white balance, flash, metering mode, and exposure compensation.

Program Shift: In P mode on the FZ20, you can rotate the dial to shift between equivalent exposures (e.g., faster shutter + wider aperture). This gives partial control.

When to use: When light is changing rapidly (street photography, event shooting) and you need speed. It's also the best mode to use while you're learning metering and white balance without worrying about exposure yet.

Practice drill: Walk around your school and shoot in P mode, but manually set white balance and ISO 80. Study the shutter/aperture the camera chooses. Start recognizing patterns.
A
Aperture Priority
You set aperture. Camera sets shutter.
What it does: You choose the aperture (f/2.8 to f/8). The camera calculates the shutter speed needed for correct exposure. ISO stays at what you set.

Why it matters: Aperture controls depth of field — how much of the scene is in focus. f/2.8 = shallow focus (background blurs). f/8 = deep focus (everything sharp).

On the FZ20: The aperture range is narrow (f/2.8–f/8), so depth of field differences are subtle. You'll see more effect at longer focal lengths (zoomed in).

When to use:
  • Architecture: f/5.6–f/8 for maximum sharpness across the frame
  • Portraits: f/2.8 for subject separation
  • Pigeons/wildlife: f/4–f/5.6 to keep subject sharp while softening background

Practice drill: Find a hallway. Shoot the same frame at f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8. Compare depth of field changes at wide and telephoto zoom positions.
S
Shutter Priority
You set shutter. Camera sets aperture.
What it does: You choose the shutter speed. The camera selects the aperture. You control motion — whether it's frozen or blurred.

When to use:
  • Trains/motion blur: 1/30s–1/8s (pan with the train)
  • Freeze birds in flight: 1/1000s–1/2000s
  • Street motion: 1/500s freezes walkers
  • Light trails (car headlights): 4–8 seconds on a surface
  • Water texture: 1/500s+ freezes spray; 1/30s or slower silks it

Watch out: If your chosen shutter speed requires an aperture smaller than f/8 or wider than f/2.8, the image will be over/underexposed. The camera will warn you. Adjust ISO or shutter speed.

Practice drill: Find a busy street or hallway. Shoot passing subjects at 1/1000s (frozen), 1/125s (slightly sharp), and 1/30s (blurred). Understand what each looks like.
M
Full Manual
You control aperture AND shutter speed.
What it does: You set both aperture and shutter speed. The camera's only job is to show you an exposure meter (a bar that tells you if your image will be over or underexposed). You interpret it and adjust.

The meter: On the FZ20's LCD/EVF, you'll see an exposure bar with 0 in the center. If the indicator is on the + side, you're overexposing. If it's on the -, you're underexposing. Aim for 0 as a starting point, then adjust for creative intent.

When to use:
  • Consistently lit scenes (golden hour, overcast days)
  • When you want the exact same exposure across multiple shots
  • Night/low light where camera guesses wrong
  • Architecture with large bright windows (manual lets you expose for the interior)

Practice workflow in M:
1. Set ISO 80
2. Set aperture for depth of field intent
3. Adjust shutter until meter reads 0
4. Evaluate image and fine-tune
🌸
Macro Mode
Close-up focus from 5cm.
What it does: Overrides the normal minimum focus distance and allows the camera to focus on subjects as close as 5cm (about 2 inches) from the lens. Without macro mode, the lens cannot focus at close range.

Focus behavior change: In macro mode, the camera optimizes AF for very close distances. It will struggle to focus on distant subjects. The depth of field at macro distances is extremely shallow — a few millimeters — meaning critical focus placement matters enormously.

When to use:
  • Architectural details: bolts, textures, lettering, patterns
  • Urban texture: worn paint, rust, concrete grain
  • Insects, flowers, any small subject
  • Creating abstract images from large structures

Important: Use wide zoom (near 35mm) for macro — the minimum focus distance increases when zoomed in. At wide angle + macro mode, you can get very close and fill the frame.

Stabilization matters here: At close focus distances, any camera movement is magnified. Brace against a wall or set camera on a surface. OIS helps but isn't enough for very long exposures.
SCN
Scene Modes
Camera presets for specific situations.
Portrait Mode: Slightly opens aperture (toward f/2.8) to blur the background. Softens skin tones. Useful for subjects against cluttered backgrounds. On the FZ20 the blur effect is mild.

Scenery/Landscape Mode: Sets a small aperture (f/8) for maximum depth of field. Saturates greens and blues. Good for skylines and wide architecture shots where you want everything sharp.

Night Mode: Uses slow shutter speeds (often 1/4s to 2s) and disables or softens flash. Designed for city lights, building exteriors at night. You need a stable surface — hand-holding will blur. Great for Cleveland/Chicago night skylines.

Night Portrait Mode: Fires flash to expose the person while keeping a slow shutter to capture background light. The flash freezes your subject but the slow shutter captures ambient light behind them.

Sport Mode: Prioritizes fast shutter speeds (1/500s+) to freeze motion. Uses burst shooting. Good for trains, birds, fast street action. The camera will raise ISO if it needs to.

When to use scene modes: When you need a quick result and know the scene type. Don't use them as a crutch — understand why each one works so you can replicate it manually.
```
Mode progression path: Start in P mode to learn metering and white balance → move to A mode to understand depth of field → move to S mode to understand motion → then use M mode deliberately when you need full control. Don't rush to Manual — it's the hardest and not always the best choice.

Commonly Overlooked Features HIDDEN VALUE

Exposure Compensation (+/- button): This is probably the most important control you're not using. Press it and rotate the dial to add or subtract up to ±2 EV of exposure in any semi-auto mode (P, A, S). Use +0.7 to +1.3 when shooting dark subjects against bright sky. Use -0.7 when shooting light subjects (white buildings, snow) to prevent blown-out whites.

Auto Bracket (ABT): Takes 3 shots in sequence at different exposures (-EV, correct, +EV). Useful when you're unsure of exposure — Cleveland architecture at golden hour, for example. Find in the burst mode button/menu.

Histogram: Press DISPLAY to cycle display modes. Find the histogram view — it shows you a graph of your image's brightness distribution. A spike on the right = blown highlights (lost detail). A spike on the left = crushed blacks. Aim for a bell curve with no clipping at either edge.

EVF vs LCD: Use the EVF (electronic viewfinder — the eyepiece) outdoors. The 2" LCD is nearly impossible to read in bright sunlight. Press EVF/LCD button to switch.

WEEK 1 Camera Control Fundamentals
DAY 1
Read the cameraSpend 30 min with camera, no shooting
Find every physical control. Press every button. Navigate every menu. Locate: ISO, WB, metering, flash, macro, OIS. Change image size to max. Turn off digital zoom. Read the EVF/LCD display symbols.
DAY 2
ISO ladderSchool hallway or outdoor wall
Shoot the same subject at ISO 80, 100, 200, 400. Same framing, same everything — only change ISO. Download to computer (or zoom in on LCD). Compare noise in shadow areas. You'll never want to use ISO 400 again.
DAY 3
Aperture ladderHallway or street
Set A mode. Shoot identical frame at f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8. At wide zoom AND telephoto zoom. 16 shots total. See how depth of field changes with both aperture and zoom. This is your depth of field reference.

Shutter speed ladderMoving subject — street, people, leaves

DAY 6
Zoom compression studySchool exterior or city block
Find a scene with depth (buildings, trees in foreground and background). Shoot it at: wide (35mm), 100mm, 200mm, 300mm, 420mm — adjusting position each time to keep subject same size. Observe how background compresses. This is telephoto compression.
DAY 7
Full review + re-shoot best frame30 min review + 30 min re-shoot

DAY 8

DAY 21

Beginner Mistakes — Specific to You & the FZ20
What goes wrong · Why · The fix
01

Leaving ISO on Auto

✗ Auto ISO quietly pushes to 400 in indoor/shade conditions. Your photos look grainy. You don't know why.
✓ Fix: Set ISO manually every session. ISO 80 outdoors, ISO 200 indoors/shade. The FZ20's CCD is noticeably degraded at 400. You need to be in control of this.
02

Using Digital Zoom

✗ You see the zoom going past 12× and think you're getting closer to your subject. You're actually cropping and destroying resolution.
✓ Fix: Turn off digital zoom in the menu. Your 12× optical zoom reaches 420mm — that's genuinely far. If you need more reach, move your feet or crop later in software.
03

Shooting Architecture at f/2.8

✗ You open to f/2.8 thinking more light = better image. But at f/2.8 with a wide lens, the front of the building is sharp but the back is slightly soft. The corner details are blurry.
✓ Fix: Architecture almost always needs f/5.6–f/8. Use the slower shutter speed this requires — brace against a wall, or use a railing as a makeshift tripod.
04

Not using Exposure Compensation

✗ Shooting buildings against bright sky and getting silhouettes or blown-out sky with dark buildings. Accepting it as "just how it is."
✓ Fix: The +/- EV compensation button is one of the most important controls on the camera. Use +0.7 to +1.3 when dark subjects are against bright sky. Use -0.3 to -0.7 for white/light subjects.
05

OIS ON during long exposure on a surface

✗ Camera is sitting still on a ledge. OIS is on. Your 2-second night shot is slightly blurred despite having the camera resting on a solid surface.
✓ Fix: When the camera is fully stationary (on a surface, not hand-held), turn OIS OFF. The stabilizer fights non-existent movement and creates micro-blur. OIS is for hand-held shooting only.
06

Shooting in Macro mode when not needed

✗ You leave macro mode on and try to photograph a building 20 meters away. The camera can't focus and gives up, or locks focus somewhere unexpected.
✓ Fix: Macro mode disables normal long-distance focusing. It's only for subjects within ~30–40cm. Check the flower icon on your display — if it's on and you're shooting far subjects, turn it off.
07

Only shooting from standing eye level

✗ Every shot is taken from 5'5"–6' height. The images feel flat, predictable, tourist-photo-like.
✓ Fix: Before pressing the shutter, ask: "Is this the most interesting angle?" Get low (ground level for pigeons, low for architecture). Go high when possible. Move side to side. The FZ20 has an EVF and LCD — you can shoot from non-standard heights.
08

Not using the EVF outdoors

✗ Holding the camera away from your face in bright sunlight trying to see a washed-out 2" LCD. Guessing at exposure and composition.
✓ Fix: Press the EVF/LCD button to switch to the eyepiece viewfinder. It's blackout-proof, more accurate for exposure, steadies the camera against your face, and extends battery life. Learn to use it as your primary view outdoors.
09

Reviewing photos between every shot

✗ You shoot, review for 10 seconds, shoot, review. You miss the moment. You kill your battery. You break your flow.
✓ Fix: Set review time to 1 second or off. Shoot your sequence, then review at the end. Trust your settings. In fast situations (trains, birds), reviewing mid-action means missing the shot.
10

Expecting DSLR depth of field from this camera

✗ Trying to get dramatically blurred backgrounds like you see in portrait photography. Setting f/2.8, shooting, feeling disappointed that backgrounds are still fairly sharp.
✓ Fix: The 1/2.5" sensor produces much less background blur than a DSLR at equivalent apertures. To maximize blur: use f/2.8, zoom in as much as possible, get as close to your subject as focus allows, and put your subject far from the background. Accept this as the camera's character, not a failure.
The one thing that separates improving photographers from stuck ones: They review their bad shots longer than their good ones. A bad shot tells you exactly what to fix. A good shot just feels nice. Learn from failure more than success.
