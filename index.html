The Exposure Triangle

Every photo is controlled by three settings that trade off against each other.

⬡
Aperture
f/2.8 → f/8 on FZ20

Controls how wide the lens opens. Lower f = more light + shallower depth of field. f/2.8 softens backgrounds. f/8 keeps everything sharp. Range is narrow on this camera — don't expect DSLR blur.

⏱
Shutter Speed
8s → 1/2000s on FZ20

Controls how long the sensor is exposed. Faster = freezes motion. Slower = blurs motion. 1/500s+ freezes birds/cats mid-air. 1/30s creates train motion blur. Below 1/60s needs a surface.

◈
ISO
ISO 80 → 400 on FZ20

Controls sensor sensitivity. Higher ISO = brighter + more grain. On this small CCD, ISO 400 is noticeably grainy. Stay at ISO 80 outdoors. ISO 200 max for important indoor shots.

Critical FZ20 limitation: The CCD sensor produces heavy color noise at ISO 400. Your best images will almost always be ISO 80–100. In low light, compensate by opening to f/2.8 and slowing the shutter — not by raising ISO.

Zoom & Image Compression

The FZ20 has 12× optical zoom (35–420mm) and 4× digital zoom. Only ever use optical. Digital zoom is just software cropping — identical to cropping in editing, but with no recovery possible.

Telephoto (toward 420mm) creates compression — backgrounds appear closer, distances look smaller, elements stack. This is a creative tool for urban shots. Wide angle (35mm) does the opposite — spaces feel large, lines converge dramatically.

Metering — How the Camera Reads Light

Default evaluative/multi-zone metering samples the whole frame and averages exposure. Works well for even lighting. Problem: dark subject against bright sky → camera exposes for sky → subject underexposes. Fix: exposure compensation (+/- button) at +1 to +2 EV, or switch to Spot Metering.

Focus Modes

9-Area AF: Default. Half-press shutter to lock focus, recompose, fully press. Essential for off-center subjects (cats at the edge of frame, buildings not centered).

Manual Focus: Use the thumb dial. EVF shows magnified preview. For macro, glass, or when AF hunts. Sliding focus switch down in MF mode fires a single AF burst — best of both worlds.

White Balance

Light has color. Daylight ≈ 5500K (blue-white). Tungsten bulbs ≈ 3200K (orange). Fluorescent ≈ greenish. AWB guesses and "corrects" this — which is bad when you want warm golden tones at sunset. Set WB to Daylight or Cloudy at golden hour to preserve warmth. For your school's fluorescent hallways, use the Fluorescent preset to kill the green cast. For window light cat shots, Daylight or Cloudy works well.

Mode Dial — Every Mode Explained
What each mode does internally · When to use it · Click to expand
AUTO
Full Auto
Camera controls everything.
What it does: Camera sets everything. You control nothing.

When to use: Almost never while learning. Fine for handing the camera to someone else. Often makes poor decisions — fires flash when you don't want it, boosts ISO unnecessarily.
P
Program AE
Camera picks exposure; you control everything else.
What it does: Camera picks shutter + aperture pair. You control ISO, WB, flash, metering, EV comp. Program Shift: rotate dial to trade between equivalent exposure pairs.

Best use: Learning WB and metering before worrying about manual exposure. Fast-changing light situations. Great for cats moving between window light and shadow.
A
Aperture Priority
You set aperture. Camera sets shutter.
When to use:
  • Architecture: f/5.6–f/8 for front-to-back sharpness
  • Black cats: f/2.8–f/4 for subtle background softening
  • Typewriter still life: f/5.6 for sharp across the object
  • Nature/flowers: f/4 balances subject sharpness with background
  • Scenic: f/8 for maximum depth

Practice drill: Shoot same object at f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8 at wide AND telephoto zoom. Build your depth of field reference.
S
Shutter Priority
You set shutter. Camera sets aperture.
When to use:
  • Freeze trains/birds: 1/1000s+
  • Freeze cat mid-jump: 1/500s+
  • Train motion blur/pan: 1/30s–1/15s
  • Light trails at night: 4–8s on a surface
  • Freeze moving water: 1/500s+
  • Silk water: 1/15s or slower
  • Freeze wind-blown leaves: 1/500s

Watch out: If shutter needs aperture beyond f/8 or below f/2.8, image over/underexposes. Camera warns you. Adjust ISO or shutter.
M
Full Manual
You set both aperture and shutter speed.
What it does: You control everything. Camera shows exposure meter bar — keep indicator near 0 for correct exposure, or shift it deliberately.

When to use:
  • Night shots on a surface
  • Cat in window — expose for fur, not bright window
  • Still life / typewriter: consistent light, consistent exposure across multiple shots
  • Any scene where camera keeps guessing wrong
🌸
Macro Mode
Close focus from 5cm. Texture, detail, cats.
What it does: Unlocks focus as close as 5cm. Without it, the lens can't focus at short distances. Depth of field at macro distances is millimeters — every bit of focus placement matters.

When to use:
  • Typewriter keys, ribbon, type mechanism
  • Cat fur, eye detail, paw texture
  • Architectural detail: bolts, worn paint, brick
  • Nature: flower stamens, leaf veins, insects

Use at wide zoom (35mm) — minimum focus distance increases as you zoom in. Brace the camera heavily.
SCN
Scene Modes
Camera presets for specific situations.
Portrait: Opens toward f/2.8, softens rendering. Cat portraits, people.
Scenery: Sets f/8, saturates colors. Nature, skylines.
Night: Slow shutter, ambient focus. City lights, cat in dim light. Needs stability.
Sport: Fast shutter, continuous AF, burst. Cats running, birds, trains.

Philosophy: Use scene modes to understand why they work — then replicate manually.
Mode progression path: P mode → learn metering/WB. A mode → learn depth of field. S mode → learn motion. M mode → full deliberate control. Don't rush to manual. It's the hardest and isn't always the right tool for every shot.

Overlooked Features With Real Value Hidden Gems

Exposure Compensation (+/- button): The most important control you're probably not using. In any semi-auto mode, add or subtract up to ±2 EV. +1 when dark subjects face bright sky. -0.7 when white cat fur or snow is blown out. -0.3 to keep black cats actually black.

Auto Bracket (ABT): Fires 3 shots: underexposed, correct, overexposed. When light is tricky — cat in window, golden-hour architecture — bracket it and pick the best.

Histogram: Press DISPLAY to cycle views. Spike to the far right = blown highlights (lost fur detail, lost sky). Spike to the far left = crushed shadows. Aim for a shape that stays within both edges.

EVF vs LCD: Press EVF/LCD button to switch. Outdoors the 2" LCD is invisible in sunlight. The EVF eyepiece is readable in any light, stabilizes the camera against your face, and saves battery. Learn to use it as your primary outdoor view.

Black Cats — The Hardest Photographic Subject

Black cats are genuinely difficult. The camera wants to overexpose them (to make them gray), and they absorb so much light that detail disappears. Here's how to beat it.

Window perch (bright light)A, f/4, ISO 80, EV -0.7 to -1.0The bright window tricks the meter. EV -0.7 darkens image so the cat doesn't become silhouette — but also keeps window from fully blowing out. Spot meter on the cat's fur.
Rim lighting (backlit)A, f/2.8, ISO 80, EV -0.3 to -0.7Let the cat go slightly silhouetted but capture the glowing rim of light around the fur. Beautiful — embrace it.
Dim roomA, f/2.8, ISO 200, WB IncandescentOpen fully to f/2.8. ISO 200 is the ceiling. Incandescent WB removes orange cast from room lights. Brace or place camera on surface.
Cat in motion (playing)S, 1/500s, ISO 200, f/2.8 forcedFast enough to freeze paw motion. Accept the grain — a sharp grainy shot beats a blurry clean one.
Eye macro🌸 Macro, A, f/5.6, ISO 80, move slowlyCat eyes in good light are stunning macro subjects. Move slowly. OIS on. Focus on the pupil edge — depth of field is millimeters.
Metering modeSpot metering — always for catsSwitch to spot metering and aim at the cat's body, not the background. Single biggest improvement for black cat photography.
Black cat reality: The camera's meter sees a dark subject and tries to make it mid-gray (it always does this to any subject it meters). Use EV -0.7 to keep the cat black, not gray. Look for catchlights — small light reflections in the eyes. They make the entire photo come alive.

WEEK 1Camera Control Fundamentals
DAY 1
Read the cameraNo shooting — navigate every menu
Find every physical control. Locate: ISO, WB, metering, flash, macro, OIS. Set image size to 5M, quality to Fine, turn off digital zoom, set review to 1 second.
DAY 2
ISO ladderSame subject, 4 ISO settings
Shoot same subject at ISO 80, 100, 200, 400. Everything else identical. Review on computer. Compare noise in shadows. You'll never want ISO 400 again.
DAY 3
Aperture ladderHallway or desk object
A mode. Same frame at f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8 — at wide zoom AND telephoto. 8 shots. Compare depth of field at each. This is your permanent reference.
DAY 4
Shutter speed ladderMoving subject — wave your hand
S mode. Same moving subject at 1/1000s, 1/250s, 1/60s, 1/15s. See what freezes vs. blurs. Your personal motion reference card.
DAY 5
White balance comparisonOutdoor + indoor scene
Same frame with AWB, Daylight, Cloudy, Incandescent, Fluorescent. Shoot near a window and under a room lamp. See color shift. Know when to override AWB.
DAY 6
Zoom compression studyStreet or building with depth
Scene with foreground and background. Shoot at wide, 100mm, 200mm, 420mm — adjusting position to keep foreground subject same size each time. Watch background compress. Use this deliberately from now on.
DAY 7
Review + re-shootBest frame from Days 2–6
Review all photos. Pick one to improve. Return to that location and re-shoot using what you learned. Compare before/after. What changed and why?
WEEK 2Exposure Mastery
DAY 8
Histogram trainingAny 20 different scenes
Enable histogram view. For each shot, predict what the histogram will look like before you check it. Learn to read exposure from the scene itself, not just the LCD image.
DAY 9
Exposure compensationBuilding vs sky · Cat by window
Shoot both scenarios at 0 EV, -1, +1, -2, +2. See exactly how + opens shadows and - protects highlights. Use this knowledge every single shoot from now on.
DAY 10
Metering modesHigh contrast scene
Backlit subject or cat near a bright window. Evaluative metering then spot metering aimed at subject vs background. The difference will be dramatic. Learn when spot metering saves you.
DAY 11
First Manual mode sessionOutdoor, consistent light
M mode only. ISO 80, f/5.6. Adjust shutter until meter reads 0. Take 5 shots. Move somewhere with different light. Re-balance meter. Repeat 4 times. Get comfortable reading the exposure bar.
DAY 12
Architecture deep diveSchool — 45 min, no auto modes
20+ shots required: 3 different focal lengths, 2 different apertures, 1 macro detail, 1 low-angle upward shot. No P or Auto. First fully deliberate architecture session.
DAY 13
Cat photography sessionWindow perch + dim room
Window perch (spot meter, EV -0.7), rim light silhouette, dim room (f/2.8 + ISO 200), macro on fur or eyes. 30+ shots. Review what worked and why it worked.
DAY 14
Golden hour shootAny outdoor location
Arrive 45 min before sunset. A, f/5.6, ISO 80, WB Cloudy. As light drops, open to f/2.8, raise ISO to 200. Document how settings must change as light fades. Most important lesson about reading light.
WEEK 3Composition & Subject Range
DAY 15
Leading linesSchool hallway / staircase
20 frames where a strong leading line is the sole compositional element. Vary position (low, high, centered, offset) for each line found. Hallways, staircases, road lanes, fence lines.
DAY 16
Rule of thirdsAny urban scene
15 shots with horizon on upper third. 15 shots on lower third. Then place main subject on each of the four grid intersection points. Compare the feel vs. dead-center framing.
DAY 17
Still life — typewriterWindow light, macro + wide
One object, 20+ shots required: full object straight-on, full object at angle, macro detail (key/dial/texture), dramatic shadow (one-sided window light + EV -0.3), background blur attempt (f/2.8 + zoom in + close).
DAY 18
Nature textures + patternsPark or green space
Macro for textures (bark, leaves, rock). Wide for landscapes. Find abstract patterns that don't look like "nature photos." Windy? Try 1/500s freeze vs 1/30s blur of moving leaves — compare both.
DAY 19
Worm's eye viewBuildings, trees, structures
Entire session from below — camera pointing upward. Buildings, staircases, trees, overhangs. All 20+ shots from below eye level. Look for converging vertical lines (keystoning) and dramatic sky framing.
DAY 20
Light and shadow onlySunny day
The shadow IS the subject. Find shadow shapes cast by railings, bars, trees, fences. Expose for highlights (EV -0.3 to -0.7). Shoot the shadow pattern, not the thing casting it. This rewires how you see light.
DAY 21
Pigeons / urban wildlifePublic square or park
45 min dedicated session. S mode, 1/1000s min, ISO 200 if needed. Eye-level ground shots, birds in flight (burst), feet/texture macro, flock behavior wide. 40+ shots, expect 3–5 keepers.
WEEK 4Portfolio Building & Advanced
DAY 22
Night shoot — city lightsDowntown Cleveland after dark
Camera on a surface (railing, ledge). Self-timer. M mode, f/2.8, ISO 200, 1–4s, OIS OFF. 3 different locations. Review for sharpness — any blur means camera moved.
DAY 23
Train/transit seriesOne station, tell the whole story
Slow panning (1/30s), frozen (1/1000s), station architecture, people waiting. Tell the story of a transit stop. 50+ shots, expect 3–5 keepers.
DAY 24
Cat portfolio shootBest light of day you've identified
Apply everything learned. Spot metering, EV compensation, f/2.8 for low light, macro for eyes. Target 5 portfolio-quality images from the session.
DAY 25
Revisit your best locationDifferent time of day than before
Return to the location that gave you the best shots. Shoot it at a completely different time. Document what changes — light direction, shadows, atmosphere. Time is a skill, not luck.
DAY 26–27
Two-day object projectOne subject, exhaustive treatment
Typewriter or another meaningful object. Day 26: every angle, window light, macro details, atmospheric shots. Day 27: different backgrounds, different light (lamp vs window), explore for black and white potential. Build a 10-image essay.
DAY 28
Full Manual only shootAny location — no semi-auto
M mode only. No A, S, or P fallback. ISO 80. React to changing light by adjusting shutter first, aperture second, ISO last. 45 minutes minimum. This is your Manual milestone.

Beginner Mistakes
What goes wrong · Why · The exact fix · FZ20-specific
01

Leaving ISO on Auto

✗ Auto ISO quietly pushes to 400 indoors. Photos are grainy. You don't know why it happened.
✓ Set ISO manually every session. ISO 80 outdoors. ISO 200 indoors. Never let the camera decide this for you.
02

Using Digital Zoom

✗ Zoom goes past 12× and you think you're getting closer. You're destroying resolution — it's identical to cropping in software, but worse.
✓ Turn off digital zoom in menu permanently. 12× optical zoom is already serious telephoto (420mm equivalent).
03

Architecture at f/2.8

✗ Opening wide for more light. But the back of the building goes soft. Corner details are blurry.
✓ Architecture almost always needs f/5.6–f/8. Brace against a wall for the slower shutter this requires.
04

Not using Exposure Compensation

✗ Building against bright sky = silhouette. White or black cat = gray. Accepting these as "just how it is."
✓ The +/- button is your most important control. +1 EV for dark subjects vs bright sky. -0.7 for any white or black subject.
05

OIS ON during long exposure on a surface

✗ Camera is sitting on a ledge. OIS is on. Your 2-second night shot is subtly blurred despite being stationary.
✓ OIS OFF for any exposure on a surface. OIS fights non-existent movement and creates micro-blur when the camera is still.
06

Not accounting for black cat metering

✗ Camera meters a dark subject and tries to make it mid-gray. Black cat becomes flat dark gray.
✓ Switch to Spot metering, aim at the cat, dial in EV -0.3 to -0.7. Keep the cat black. Look for catchlights in the eyes — they make the whole photo.
07

Macro mode left on after close-up

✗ Macro mode stays on. You try to shoot the street. Camera can't focus and locks on the wrong thing.
✓ Macro mode disables normal long-distance focus. Check for the flower icon. Turn it off immediately after close-up work.
08

Only shooting from standing eye level

✗ Every photo is from 5'5"–6' height. Images feel flat and predictable.
✓ Before pressing shutter, ask: is this the most interesting angle? Get low for cats and pigeons. Shoot up for buildings. Height is a choice, not a default.
09

Not using the EVF outdoors

✗ Holding camera at arm's length trying to see a washed-out LCD in sunlight. Guessing at composition and exposure.
✓ Press EVF/LCD button to switch to the eyepiece. Readable in all light. Steadies the camera. Saves battery. Use it as your primary outdoor viewfinder.
10

AWB killing warm tones at golden hour

✗ Auto white balance "corrects" the warm sunset glow and makes it look like noon. The golden magic disappears.
✓ Set WB to Daylight or Cloudy at golden hour. AWB is actively working against you in warm light. Fastest way to improve sunset shots.
11

Expecting DSLR background blur

✗ Setting f/2.8, feeling disappointed that backgrounds are still fairly sharp compared to portrait photography you see online.
✓ The 1/2.5" CCD produces much less blur than a DSLR. Maximize by using f/2.8 + telephoto zoom + close to subject + subject far from background. Accept this as the camera's character, not a failure.
The habit that separates improving photographers from stuck ones: They review their bad shots longer than their good ones. A bad shot tells you exactly what to change. A good shot just feels nice. Study failure more than success.
