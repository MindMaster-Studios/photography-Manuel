The Exposure Triangle

Every photo is controlled by three settings that trade off against each other.

⬡
Aperture
f/2.8 → f/8 on FZ20

Controls lens opening. Lower f = more light + shallower depth of field. f/2.8 softens backgrounds. f/8 keeps everything sharp. The FZ20's range is narrow — expect subtle, not dramatic blur.

⏱
Shutter Speed
8s → 1/2000s on FZ20

Controls how long the sensor is exposed. Faster = freezes motion. Slower = blurs motion. 1/500s+ freezes cats/birds mid-jump. 1/30s creates motion blur on trains. Below 1/60s needs support.

◈
ISO
ISO 80 → 400 on FZ20

Controls sensor sensitivity. Higher ISO = brighter + more grain. On this small CCD, ISO 400 is noticeably grainy. Stay at ISO 80 outdoors. ISO 200 max for important low-light shots.

Critical FZ20 limitation: The CCD produces heavy color noise at ISO 400. Your best images will almost always be ISO 80–100. In low light, compensate by opening to f/2.8 and slowing the shutter — not by raising ISO.

Zoom & Compression

The FZ20 has 12× optical zoom (35–420mm) and 4× digital zoom. Only use optical. Digital zoom is just cropping — identical to cropping in editing later, except you can't undo it.

Telephoto (toward 420mm) creates compression — backgrounds appear closer, elements stack dramatically. Great for urban shots and nature layers. Wide angle (35mm) makes spaces feel larger, lines converge. Good for interiors, hallways, and dramatic architecture.

Metering — How the Camera Reads Light

Default evaluative metering samples the whole frame and averages it. Works well for even light. Problem: a bright sky causes it to underexpose your building. A black cat gets pushed to gray. A white object gets pushed to gray too — the camera always aims for mid-gray.

Fix: use exposure compensation (+/- button). Add +EV for dark subjects against bright backgrounds. Subtract -EV for very light or very dark subjects to keep them accurate.

Focus Modes

9-Area AF: Default. Samples multiple zones. Works for most situations.
Spot AF: Focuses only on the center. Use for a cat's eye, object detail, or any time 9-area picks the wrong thing.
Manual Focus (MF): You control focus with the dial. EVF/LCD magnifies to help. Use for macro, through glass, or low-contrast scenes where AF hunts.
Focus lock: Half-press shutter to lock AF on your subject, then recompose, then fully press. Essential for off-center subjects.

White Balance

Light has color. Sunlight is blue-white. Tungsten bulbs are orange-warm. Fluorescent is greenish. AWB guesses and corrects.

When AWB fights you: At golden hour, AWB neutralizes the warm glow you actually want — set WB to Daylight or Cloudy to preserve it. Shooting cats or objects under your room's lamp, try Incandescent to remove the orange cast. In school hallways, try the Fluorescent preset.

Mode Dial — Every Mode Explained
What each mode does internally · When to use it · Click to expand
SIMPLE
Simple Mode FZ20 NOTE
The FZ20's closest thing to full auto.
Important: The FZ20 does not have a dedicated "AUTO" position on the mode dial. Some versions of the camera have a "Simple" mode (sometimes an icon, not text) that locks everything and limits menus to basics. Other dial positions you might confuse with auto are P (Program AE) — which is the practical equivalent of a smart auto with more control.

Your actual dial positions are: Simple (if present), P, A, S, M, SCN (Scene modes), and Motion Picture.

What to use instead of AUTO: Use P mode. It handles exposure automatically while still letting you set ISO, WB, and EV compensation — meaning you learn something every time you use it instead of nothing.

If you've been shooting in a mode that "just works": That's probably P mode. Good starting point — now start overriding its defaults.
P
Program AE
Camera picks exposure, you pick everything else.
What it does: Camera auto-selects shutter + aperture combo for correct exposure. You control ISO, WB, flash, metering, and EV compensation. Program Shift: rotate the dial to shift to equivalent exposures (faster shutter + wider aperture).

When to use: Rapidly changing light. Also the best mode while learning WB and ISO without worrying about exposure yet.

Practice: Shoot around school in P mode, but manually set WB and ISO 80. Watch what shutter/aperture the camera selects. Learn its patterns.
A
Aperture Priority
You set aperture. Camera sets shutter.
What it does: You choose the aperture (f/2.8 to f/8). Camera calculates shutter speed.

Key uses:
  • Architecture: f/5.6–f/8 → max sharpness throughout
  • Cats: f/2.8 → softest background, most light
  • Nature/scenic: f/5.6–f/8 → everything sharp foreground to sky
  • Objects: f/4–f/5.6 → slight separation, still sharp
  • People: f/2.8–f/4 → gentle background softening
Practice: Shoot same hallway frame at f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8 at both wide and telephoto zoom. Build your depth-of-field reference.
S
Shutter Priority
You set shutter. Camera sets aperture.
What it does: You choose the shutter speed. Camera selects aperture. You control how motion appears.

Key uses:
  • Freeze birds/cats/trains: 1/1000s–1/2000s
  • Freeze running cats: 1/500s
  • Motion blur on trains: 1/30s–1/8s
  • Light trails at night: 4–8s on a surface
  • Silky water: 1/4s or slower
Watch out: If shutter requires an aperture outside f/2.8–f/8, you'll get wrong exposure. Camera will warn you.
M
Full Manual
You control both aperture AND shutter.
What it does: You set everything. Camera shows an exposure meter bar — adjust until indicator sits near 0. Then adjust creatively from there.

When to use:
  • Consistently lit scenes — indoor cat shots under lamp
  • Night/long exposure — camera guesses wrong in the dark
  • Same exposure locked across multiple shots of the same subject
  • Typewriter mixed lamp/window light
Workflow: Set ISO → Set aperture for DOF intent → Adjust shutter until meter reads ~0 → Evaluate → Fine-tune
🌸
Macro Mode
Close-up focus from 5cm.
What it does: Allows focus as close as 5cm. Without it, lens can't focus on nearby subjects.

Use at wide zoom (35mm): Minimum focus distance is smallest at wide angle. Zooming in increases that distance again. Get in close at wide zoom.

Key uses:
  • Cat's eyes and fur texture
  • Typewriter keys, ribbon, metal detail
  • Architectural texture — bolts, rust, worn paint
  • Nature — insects, leaf texture, patterns
Critical: Macro disables long-distance focusing. Check for the flower icon — if on and shooting far away, turn it off.
SCN
Scene Modes
Presets for specific conditions.
Portrait: Opens toward f/2.8, softens background. Subtle on the FZ20.
Scenery/Landscape: Sets f/8, saturates greens/blues. Great for nature and wide skylines.
Night: Slow shutter, ambient light capture. Great for city lights. Needs a surface.
Night Portrait: Flash + slow shutter. Freezes subject while capturing background light.
Sport: Fast shutter, burst mode, raises ISO. Good for birds, jumping cats, trains.

Understand the why: Know what aperture/shutter/ISO each scene mode is actually using so you can replicate it manually later.
Mode progression: P mode to learn metering and WB → A mode for depth of field → S mode for motion → M mode for full deliberate control. Don't rush to Manual — use it when you actually need it.

Commonly Overlooked Features HIDDEN VALUE

Exposure Compensation (+/- button): Your most important control. In any semi-auto mode (P, A, S), press it and dial in ±EV. Use +0.7 to +1.3 for dark subjects against bright sky. Use -0.3 to -0.7 for black cat fur (Lincoln in low light) and for white objects — the meter will make both gray without your correction.

Auto Bracket (ABT): Takes 3 shots at -EV, correct, +EV automatically. Use for tricky indoor cat shots or architectural lighting where you're unsure.

Histogram: Press DISPLAY to cycle. Histogram = your image's brightness graph. Spike at far right = blown highlights. Spike at far left = crushed blacks. For Lincoln: bulk of tones should sit left of center, not centered. For Batman: similar but slightly less extreme.

EVF vs LCD: Press EVF/LCD to switch to the eyepiece. Use outdoors where 2" LCD is unreadable. Also steadies the camera — your face becomes a tripod brace.

Self-Timer (2s): Use for any camera-on-surface shot. Eliminates the camera shake from pressing the shutter button. Essential for night/long exposures.

For "random shit" that catches your eye: Keep camera in P mode with WB set (not AWB) as your quick-grab baseline. Capture the moment first, refine technique second. The instinct to notice something is the most important skill in photography — don't miss it fumbling with settings.
Why your dead tree / sunset shot was blurry — diagnosed: At sunset, light is low. If you were zoomed in toward telephoto to frame the tree against the sky, the camera dropped the shutter to something like 1/15s or slower. At 200–420mm zoom, even a genuinely steady hand causes blur below 1/125s — telephoto magnifies every micro-tremor, and OIS can't fully compensate in dim light.

The fix for hand-held sunset + telephoto: Raise ISO to 200 before shooting so the camera has headroom to keep shutter fast. In S mode, explicitly lock 1/250s. In A or P mode, check what shutter speed the camera selected on the display before you press — if it's below roughly 1/zoom (below 1/300 at 300mm zoom), raise ISO until the shutter comes up. Bracing technique also matters: elbows tucked in against your body, exhale slowly, squeeze the shutter rather than jabbing it.

Lincoln & Batman — Black Cats, Every Lighting Condition

Black cats are one of the hardest subjects to expose correctly. The camera always wants to make them gray. Your job is to keep them black while showing enough detail in the fur.

Exposure for black subjects: The camera sees black fur and tries to push it to mid-gray. Dial -0.3 to -0.7 EV to keep Lincoln actually black. Check histogram — bulk of tones should sit left of center, not centered.

Beginner Mistakes
What goes wrong · Why · The exact fix · FZ20-specific
01

Leaving ISO on Auto

✗ Auto ISO quietly pushes to 400 indoors. Photos are grainy. You don't know why.
✓ Set ISO manually every session. ISO 80 outdoors. ISO 200 indoors. Never let the camera decide this for you.
02

Using Digital Zoom

✗ Zoom goes past 12× and you think you're getting closer. You're destroying resolution — identical to cropping in software, except irreversible.
✓ Turn off digital zoom permanently. 12× optical (420mm equiv.) is already serious reach.
03

Architecture at f/2.8

✗ Opening wide for more light. But far parts of the building go soft. Corner details blur.
✓ Architecture needs f/5.6–f/8. Brace against a wall for the slower shutter this requires.
04

Not using Exposure Compensation

✗ Building against bright sky = silhouette. Black cat = flat gray. White typewriter keys = dull gray. You accept it as "just how it came out."
✓ The +/- button is your most-used control. +EV for dark subjects vs bright backgrounds. -EV for any pure black or white subject to maintain accuracy.
05

OIS ON during long exposure on a surface

✗ Camera on a ledge. OIS on. 2-second night shot is subtly blurred despite the solid surface.
✓ OIS OFF for any exposure on a surface. OIS fights non-existent movement and creates micro-blur. OIS is hand-held only.
06

Black cats metering to gray

✗ Camera meters Lincoln's black fur and tries to make it mid-gray. Black cat becomes flat dark gray.
✓ Spot meter on the cat's body, then dial -0.3 to -0.7 EV. Keep him black. Look for the catchlight in the eye — one tiny bright reflection transforms the whole shot.
07

Macro mode left on after close-up

✗ Macro stays on. You try to shoot the street. Camera can't focus and locks somewhere random.
✓ Check for the flower icon. Macro disables normal long-distance focus. Turn it off immediately after close-up work.
08

Only shooting from standing eye level

✗ Every photo from 5'5"–6' height. Flat and predictable. Tourist-photo energy.
✓ Before pressing shutter, ask: is this the most interesting angle? Get flat on the ground for cats and birds. Shoot up at buildings. Height is a choice, not a default.
09

Not using the EVF outdoors

✗ Arm's length in sunlight trying to see a washed-out LCD. Guessing at everything.
✓ Press EVF/LCD to switch to the eyepiece. Readable in any light. Steadies the camera. Extends battery. Use it as your primary outdoor viewfinder.
10

AWB killing warm tones at golden hour

✗ AWB "corrects" the warm sunset and makes it look like noon. The golden magic disappears.
✓ Set WB to Daylight or Cloudy at golden hour. AWB is actively working against you in warm light.
11

Expecting DSLR background blur

✗ Setting f/2.8, feeling disappointed that backgrounds are still fairly sharp.
✓ Maximize blur: f/2.8 + fully zoomed in + close to subject + subject far from background. Accept the 1/2.5" CCD's limitations as the camera's character, not a failure.
12

Wrong WB under artificial light

✗ Shooting cats or objects indoors with AWB or Daylight WB. Everything is orange under lamps, or green under school fluorescents.
✓ Incandescent WB for lamps. Fluorescent WB for school/office tubes. Set it deliberately — don't let AWB guess at indoor light.
The habit that separates improving photographers from stuck ones: They review their bad shots longer than their good ones. A bad shot tells you exactly what to change. A good shot just feels nice. Study failure more than success.
Lightroom Editing Tutorial
What every tool does · How to use it · FZ20 JPEG workflow · Your subjects specifically
Important context for FZ20 files: Your camera shoots JPEG only — not RAW. This means some editing headroom is lost compared to RAW files. JPEGs are already processed by the camera. You can still do a lot, but be gentler with aggressive adjustments — especially Shadows and Highlights — because JPEG doesn't hold up as well as RAW when pushed hard. Work with finesse, not force.

The Complete Edit Order — All 13 Steps START HERE

Editing in the wrong order creates compounding problems. Global corrections before creative decisions. Light before color. Color before detail. Detail before local adjustments. Always.

Step 1Crop & StraightenFix composition before touching anything else. Cropping changes what you're looking at — don't edit pixels you're about to throw away. Use the R key shortcut to open crop. Straighten with the angle tool or hold Cmd/Ctrl and drag a line along the horizon.
Step 2White Balance — Temp & Tint ONLYThis means only the Temp and Tint sliders (or the eyedropper) in the WB section at the top of the Basic panel. NOT Vibrance or Saturation — those are in the Presence section below and come much later. Fix the overall color cast here first. A warm or cool cast will make every other adjustment misleading. Use the eyedropper on a neutral surface, or drag Temp until the image looks correctly lit.
Step 3ExposureRough overall brightness. Get it close — don't spend time perfecting it yet. Just ballpark the image so you can see what you're working with. You'll revisit this after highlights and shadows are set.
Step 4Highlights & ShadowsRecover any blown highlights (pull Highlights down) and open up dark areas (push Shadows up). For FZ20 JPEGs stay within ±50 or you'll get banding and color noise. These two sliders compress the dynamic range — essential for high-contrast shots like buildings against bright sky.
Step 5Whites & BlacksSet the actual white and black points of the image. Hold Alt/Option while dragging — screen goes black/white to show exactly which pixels are clipping. Drag Whites up until just before the first clipping. Drag Blacks down until just before shadow clipping. This is how you get a full tonal range with no wasted headroom.
Step 6Contrast / Tone CurveAdd depth and punch after the range is set. The Contrast slider is a quick S-curve applied globally. The Tone Curve gives you more control — add points in the upper-third (pull up) and lower-third (pull down) for a manual S-curve. FZ20 JPEGs benefit from +15–25 Contrast as a baseline before touching the curve.
Step 7Texture, Clarity, DehazeAdd or reduce mid-frequency detail. Texture enhances fine surface detail (fur, brick, keys) — natural and safe at +10 to +25. Clarity adds contrast at edges — great for architecture and objects, use sparingly on faces. Dehaze cuts atmospheric haze and adds saturation — use +5 to +15 for flat overcast shots.
Step 8Vibrance & SaturationThis is where Vibrance and Saturation live — NOT in the WB step. After all your light work is done, global color intensity comes here. Vibrance (+15 to +25) is almost always better than Saturation — it protects already-vivid colors and skin tones. Use Saturation only for fine-tuning. Do this AFTER color work, not before.
Step 9HSL — Individual ColorsAdjust specific color channels independently. Hue (shifts what shade a color is), Saturation (intensity per color), Luminance (brightness per color). Lower Blue Luminance to darken skies. Lower Orange Saturation to reduce overly warm skin. Boost Green Saturation for nature shots. HSL comes after global saturation is set so you're fine-tuning from a correct baseline.
Step 10Color Grading — Three WheelsNow the creative color work. Shadows wheel, Midtones wheel, Highlights wheel — push these toward complementary colors to create your signature split tone. Teal shadows + orange highlights for urban work. Cool shadows + warm highlights for golden hour. This layer goes after HSL because color grading builds on top of corrected individual colors, not instead of them.
Step 11Sharpening & Noise ReductionDetail panel — sharpen after all tonal and color work is done so you're sharpening the final image. Amount 40–60 for FZ20 JPEGs. Hold Alt/Option while dragging Masking to only sharpen edges. Color NR +25 for virtually every FZ20 shot — the CCD generates color noise that this immediately improves. Check at 1:1 (100%) zoom.
Step 12Masking — Local AdjustmentsNow and only now do local/selective edits. Global corrections are locked in — masking refines specific areas on top of that correct base. Start with AI masks (Select Subject, Select Sky) for speed. Add manual brush work to clean up edges or hit specific details (cat eyes, typewriter keys, window glow). If masking feels like you're fighting the image, go back and fix a global setting instead.
Step 13Vignette & Final CheckAdd a subtle vignette in the Effects panel (-10 to -20, Feather 85) to draw the eye inward. Then use the backslash key (\) to toggle before/after 3–5 times. Ask honestly: does every adjustment serve the photo? If something feels forced, reduce it by 30%. Export when the before/after toggle makes you confident.
About White Balance in Lightroom — where things actually are: The WB section in the Basic panel has Temp and Tint only. Those are Step 2. Directly below WB in the same Basic panel is the Presence section which has Texture, Clarity, Dehaze, Vibrance, and Saturation — those are Steps 7 and 8. They look close together in the UI but they serve completely different purposes and belong at different points in your workflow. Don't touch Vibrance/Saturation when you're working on white balance.
The Alt/Option clipping trick for Whites and Blacks: Hold Alt (Windows) or Option (Mac) while dragging either the Whites or Blacks slider. The entire screen goes black (for Whites) or white (for Blacks). Any colored or white/black areas that appear are pixels clipping — losing detail permanently. Stop dragging the instant the first colored pixel appears. This is how professionals set a perfect tonal range every time.

The Light Panel — Every Slider Explained

Exposure±2 EV typical rangeThe overall brightness dial. Think of it like the shutter speed you should have set in camera. Moving +1 roughly doubles brightness, -1 halves it. For FZ20 JPEGs, don't push beyond ±1.5 or quality degrades visibly.
Contrast+10 to +30 typicalPushes darks darker and lights lighter simultaneously. Adds punch. FZ20 JPEGs often look a bit flat straight out of camera — a small contrast boost (+15–25) is almost always an improvement.
Highlights-20 to -80 for recoveryPulls down only the bright areas (clouds, sky, bright windows). Negative values recover detail in blown highlights. On JPEGs, recovery is limited — you're working with what was captured. If the sky is pure white in the file, no amount of Highlights slider will bring back clouds that weren't recorded.
Shadows+10 to +50 for liftingLifts only the dark areas — reveals shadow detail without blowing out bright areas. Great for lifting detail in dark building interiors while keeping the sky correctly exposed. On FZ20 JPEGs, be careful above +50 — you may see noise revealed or color banding in the shadows.
WhitesSet to just before clippingDefines the brightest point in the image. Raising Whites brightens highlights without the hard cap that Exposure has. Hold Alt/Option while dragging to see exactly when highlights clip. For most images, set so the histogram just touches the right edge without piling up.
Blacks-10 to -30 for depthDefines the darkest point. Lowering Blacks deepens shadows and adds richness. Hold Alt/Option while dragging to see when pure black appears. For urban/architecture shots, -15 to -25 adds tremendous depth and makes colors pop.

Tone Curve — More Precise Than Contrast

The Tone Curve is a graph where the horizontal axis is input brightness (dark on left, bright on right) and the vertical axis is output brightness. You add points and drag them to reshape how tones are rendered.

Classic S-curve: Add a point in the upper third and pull up slightly (brightens highlights). Add a point in the lower third and pull down slightly (deepens shadows). This creates an S-shape that adds contrast and punch while retaining more midtone detail than the Contrast slider. This is the single most useful curve shape for your photos.

For FZ20 JPEG: Keep curve adjustments subtle — small S-curves. Aggressive curves on JPEG can posterize (create bands of flat tone instead of smooth gradation). The Point Curve is powerful but less forgiving on compressed files.

The four regions: Highlights (top right), Lights (upper middle), Darks (lower middle), Shadows (bottom left). You can drag each of these named sliders in Lightroom's Parametric Curve section instead of free-placing points — easier for beginners.

White Balance — Exactly What's in the WB Section CLARIFIED

The WB section in Lightroom's Basic panel contains only two sliders: Temp and Tint. That's it. Vibrance and Saturation are NOT part of white balance — they're in the Presence section directly below it and are used at a completely different stage (Step 8 in your workflow, not Step 2).

Temp sliderBlue (left) ←→ Orange (right)Adjusts color temperature in Kelvin. Drag right to warm the image (more orange/yellow). Drag left to cool it (more blue). For golden hour shots, try pushing Temp right (+200 to +500 from the As Shot value) to lean into the warmth. For indoor lamp shots of the cats, push right until the orange cast feels natural rather than sickly. For overcast outdoor, slight right push (+100–200) removes the cold blue cast.
Tint sliderGreen (left) ←→ Magenta (right)Corrects green or magenta color casts that Temp alone can't fix. Fluorescent lights (school hallways) add green — push right toward magenta (+5 to +15). Old incandescent bulbs sometimes add slight green — same fix. For outdoor natural light, Tint rarely needs more than ±5. If you used the WB eyedropper and the image still looks off, Tint is usually the culprit.
WB EyedropperClick any neutral gray or white surfaceThe fastest and most accurate WB fix. Click it on something in the frame that should be neutral — gray concrete, white paper, a gray wall, black asphalt. Lightroom calculates and sets both Temp and Tint simultaneously. Works best for indoor artificial light shots (typewriter, cats under lamps). If the result looks wrong, the surface you clicked wasn't truly neutral — try a different spot.
WB Presets dropdownAs Shot / Daylight / Cloudy / etc.Click the dropdown next to "WB:" to apply a named preset. "As Shot" uses whatever WB your camera had set. "Daylight" applies a standard daylight temperature. "Cloudy" warms slightly. For FZ20 JPEGs, "As Shot" is your starting point — then adjust Temp manually from there.
Vibrance and Saturation are NOT white balance. They're in the Presence section immediately below WB in the Basic panel. They control color intensity, not color accuracy. Don't touch them during the WB step. Use them at Step 8 after all your light, contrast, and color grading is complete. The proximity in the UI is confusing — they're not related to WB at all.

HSL Panel — Individual Color Control

HSL (Hue, Saturation, Luminance) lets you adjust each color channel independently. This is Step 9 — after global saturation is set. Find it by scrolling down past Color Grading in the Develop module and clicking on "Color" or "HSL/Color."

HueShifts the shade of a colorChanges what a color actually looks like. Orange Hue toward red makes warm tones redder. Blue Hue toward cyan shifts sky toward teal. Yellow Hue shifts it toward green or orange. Use sparingly — obvious hue shifts look unnatural fast. Best use: shifting Yellow toward Green to make grass look greener, or Blue toward Cyan for a more vivid sky.
SaturationIntensity per colorBoost Blues for a deeper sky. Boost Orange for warmer sunset tones. Pull Orange down if skin looks too orange. This is more precise than the global Vibrance/Saturation sliders because it only affects one color. Common moves: Blue +15–25 for skies, Orange +10 for warmth, Green +10 for nature.
LuminanceBrightness per colorThe most underused slider. Lower Blue Luminance (-15 to -25) darkens the sky dramatically without changing its saturation — one of the best moves for moody architecture shots. Lower Orange Luminance slightly (-10) deepens warm tones. Raise Aqua/Teal Luminance to brighten shadowed architecture that has a blue cast.
Targeted Adjustment ToolClick and drag directly on imageIn the HSL panel, click the small circle icon (top-left of the panel) to activate the Targeted Adjustment Tool. Then click and drag UP or DOWN directly on a color in your photo. Lightroom automatically adjusts the correct Hue/Saturation/Luminance channel for that color. This is the fastest way to darken a sky, deepen warm tones, or fix a specific color cast.

Vibrance vs Saturation — Step 8, After All Color Work

Saturation: Boosts ALL colors equally and aggressively. Above +30 it quickly makes images look cartoonish and blows out already-vivid colors. Use it for fine adjustments of ±5–15, not as a primary color boost.

Vibrance: Intelligently boosts muted/undersaturated colors while protecting already-vivid colors and skin tones. For almost all photos, use Vibrance instead of Saturation as your primary tool. +15 to +25 Vibrance is a solid starting point for most images.

FZ20-specific: FZ20 JPEGs tend to be slightly undersaturated in overcast light. In overcast: +Vibrance +15–25. In direct sun: -Saturation -5 to -10 to prevent oranges from clipping before you boost other areas.

Sharpening — Making the FZ20 Look Its Best

The FZ20's CCD sensor and Leica lens actually produce quite sharp images in good light. In-camera JPEG sharpening is already applied. In Lightroom, you're adding a second layer — be careful not to oversharpen, which creates halos around edges and emphasizes noise.

Amount40–60 for FZ20 JPEGsHow much sharpening is applied. FZ20 JPEGs are already sharpened in camera. 40–60 adds a gentle additional layer. Above 80 will create visible halos and emphasize the CCD's noise.
Radius0.8–1.2How wide the sharpening halo extends around edges. For the FZ20's pixel density, keep this at 1.0 or below. Higher values sharpen broader areas and look artificial.
Detail25–50Controls whether sharpening hits fine texture (high values) or only major edges (low values). For architecture and objects (typewriter), go up to 50. For cats and portraits, stay lower — you don't want to sharpen every individual fur strand.
Masking20–60 for most shotsThe most important sharpening slider. Hold Alt/Option while dragging — the image goes grayscale and shows you exactly where sharpening will be applied (white = sharpened, black = protected). Drag until only edges and textures are white, smooth surfaces are black. This prevents sharpening flat sky or skin, where it only amplifies noise.
Sharpening hack: Always check sharpening at 1:1 (100%) zoom, not at fit view. At fit view the image looks sharp regardless. Zoom in to 100% and check for halos around high-contrast edges — if you see white outlines, reduce Amount or increase Masking.

Noise Reduction — Fixing ISO 200+ Shots

The FZ20's CCD shows noticeable luminance noise (grain) and color noise (random colored specks) above ISO 200. Lightroom's noise reduction is excellent even on JPEGs.

Luminance NR15–30 for ISO 200Smooths out the grain/texture noise. The tradeoff is softness — high values make images look plasticky. For ISO 200 FZ20 shots: 15–25 is the sweet spot. Increase Detail slider within NR to preserve some texture while reducing noise.
Color NR25–40 defaultRemoves the random colored speckles (red/green/blue dots in shadows). The FZ20's CCD is prone to color noise in shadows even at ISO 100. Start at 25 — it's almost always an improvement. Unlike Luminance NR, Color NR has little quality cost at moderate values.
Sharpening + NR balanceAlways apply NR before final sharpenApply noise reduction first, then check if you still need sharpening and apply it on top. Sharpening applied before NR will sharpen the noise itself, making it worse.

Masking & Local Adjustments — The Real Power

Global adjustments affect the entire image. Local adjustments let you edit specific areas. These are what separate beginner edits from professional ones.

Graduated Filter (Linear Gradient)

Applies adjustments that fade across the image in a straight line. The most common use: darken a bright sky without affecting the ground.

How to use: Select Graduated Filter → drag from the top of the image downward to set the gradient → set Exposure -0.5 to -1.5 and/or Highlights -50 to pull the sky down while leaving the foreground alone. You can also add a blue Temp shift to make skies cooler and more dramatic.

Your use case — dead tree at sunset: Pull a graduated filter from the top down to darken and saturate the sky separately from the tree. Add a second gradient from the bottom up to slightly lift foreground shadows.

Radial Filter (Radial Gradient)

Applies adjustments inside or outside an ellipse. The best tool for: drawing attention to a subject, creating vignette-like effects with precision, brightening a cat's face without brightening the whole image.

How to use: Select Radial Filter → draw an oval around your subject → invert the mask (so the inside of the oval is affected) → brighten Exposure +0.3–0.5, boost Clarity +10–20. The edges of the oval automatically feather, so the effect blends naturally.

For Lincoln/Batman: Draw a radial filter oval around the cat → inside: boost Exposure +0.3, Shadows +20, Texture +15. This lifts the cat's detail without blowing the background. Add another radial filter tightly around the eye area and push Clarity +20 for sharper, more defined eyes.

Brush Tool — Paint Adjustments Anywhere

Lets you paint edits onto any specific area with a brush. The most flexible local tool.

How to use: Select Brush → set Size, Feather, Flow → paint over the area you want to affect → adjust sliders for that area only.

Best uses for your photos:

  • Typewriter: Paint Clarity +20–30 onto the metal keys for extra texture and definition. Paint Shadows +20 on darker areas to reveal detail.
  • Architecture: Paint Shadows +15–25 onto the shadowed underside of a building separately from the sunlit face.
  • Cats — eye enhancement: Very small brush, paint Clarity +25, Exposure +0.3 onto each eye. This is subtle but makes a huge difference.
  • Reducing distracting elements: Paint Exposure -0.5 onto a background object that's too bright and drawing attention away from your subject.

Vignette (Effects Panel)

Darkens the corners and edges of the image, drawing the eye toward the center. One of the most overused effects — use subtly.

Amount: -10 to -25 is tasteful. Below -40 looks heavy-handed unless you're going for an intentional old-photograph look.
Midpoint: How far the vignette extends toward the center. Higher midpoint = tighter, more concentrated darkening at corners.
Feather: How soft the transition is. Always keep this high (80–100) unless you want a hard oval border effect.
Roundness: Shape of the vignette — 0 is rectangular, +100 is circular.

For FZ20 shots: A subtle vignette (-10 to -20, Feather 85) works well for architecture, cats on a perch, and objects like the typewriter. It makes the subject feel contained without being obvious.

Clarity vs Texture vs Dehaze

These three are often confused. They all add "punch" but in very different ways.

Texture (+10 to +25): Enhances fine mid-frequency details — fur, brick texture, typewriter metal, fabric, bark. Subtle and natural-looking. Great for everything. Rarely hurts a photo at moderate values.

Clarity (+10 to +30): Adds contrast at medium-sized edges — makes objects feel crisp and substantial. Great for architecture, objects, landscapes. Bad for portraits and cats at high values — makes every pore and fur strand look harsh. Use on subjects, not faces.

Dehaze (+5 to +20): Removes atmospheric haze — increases contrast and saturation simultaneously. Good for distant cityscapes, foggy architecture, flat overcast shots. Easy to overdo — above +30 it makes images look artificially crunchy. Also useful in reverse (-10 to -20) to add a soft hazy/dreamy look.

Color Grading
The three color wheels · How to use them · Looks for your subjects · Color theory for photographers
Color grading vs color correction: Color correction is fixing problems — removing a green cast, getting white balance accurate. Color grading is intentional creative choice — making shadows go teal, warming the highlights, creating a cinematic look. You always correct first, then grade. Think of correction as making the photo accurate, and grading as making it yours.

Where to Find the Color Grading Panel

In Lightroom's Develop module, scroll down past HSL/Color to find Color Grading. You'll see three circular color wheels labeled Shadows, Midtones, and Highlights, plus a central dot and a luminance slider under each. There's also a fourth "Global" wheel that affects everything at once. Click the three-circle icon at the top to see all three wheels simultaneously.

Shadows
Darkest 25% of tones
Controls the color tint applied to the darkest parts of your image — deep blacks, dark corners, shadowed areas of buildings, the dark fur on Lincoln.

How to use: Drag the center dot toward a color to tint the shadows that color. Drag it toward blue/teal to make shadows cooler. Push toward orange/amber for warm shadows. The farther from center you drag, the stronger the effect.

Luminance slider (under the wheel): Raises or lowers the overall brightness of the shadows independently of color. Different from the Shadows slider in the Light panel — this one applies after your color grade.
Midtones
Middle 50% of tones
Controls the color in the middle brightness range — most of your subject matter lives here. Brick walls, faces, cat fur, sky at mid-exposure, architecture detail.

How to use: This wheel has the most visible impact because midtones are the bulk of any image. Subtle moves go a long way — drag just 5–10% from center to start. Strong midtone color shifts define an image's overall mood.

This is your primary creative wheel. More often than not, your shadow and highlight tweaks are supporting the look you set here first.
Highlights
Brightest 25% of tones
Controls color in the brightest areas — sky, clouds, sun-lit surfaces, bright windows, light-colored walls.

How to use: Push toward warm/yellow-orange to make bright areas feel golden and sunny. Push toward cool/blue-white for a crisp, clean, airy feel. This wheel pairs with Shadows to create split-toning — the single most powerful color grading technique.

Keep moves subtle here — bright areas show color shifts very easily. A tiny pull toward orange or teal in highlights is often all you need.

The Center Dot — How Dragging Actually Works

Each wheel has a small dot in the center. Dragging the dot toward any edge pushes that color into the tonal range. The direction you drag determines the hue. The distance from center determines the intensity.

Important — the dot starts at dead center (neutral). If you drag it and the image looks wrong, drag it back to center to reset. You can also double-click the dot to reset it to neutral.

The luminance slider below each wheel is separate from the color — it only adjusts the brightness of that tonal range without changing the color tint you've applied. Use it after you've set the color to fine-tune the overall brightness balance.

Split Toning — The Most Useful Technique

Split toning means applying one color to shadows and a different (usually opposite) color to highlights. The contrast between cool shadows and warm highlights — or vice versa — is what gives photos that "film" or "cinematic" quality that looks intentional rather than accidental.

The classic split tone: Shadows → push toward blue/teal. Highlights → push toward orange/yellow. This is the most widely used color grade in photography and film. It works because warm and cool are complementary — they contrast each other and create visual depth.

Why it works on your photos: Urban architecture has lots of both warm surfaces (brick, concrete in sun) and cool shadows (under overhangs, alleyways). Split toning amplifies these natural contrasts rather than fighting them.

Color Theory You Actually Need to Know

You don't need to memorize color theory textbooks. You need three concepts: complementary colors, temperature, and what different colors feel like emotionally.

Complementary Colors — The Foundation of All Color Grading

Complementary colors sit opposite each other on the color wheel. They create maximum contrast when placed together, which is why they look so visually satisfying.

TEAL ↔ ORANGE
The most popular grade in cinema. Teal shadows + orange skin/warmth. Works especially well for urban + portrait shots.
BLUE ↔ YELLOW
Cool blue shadows + warm golden highlights. Classic for architecture, golden hour, and moody cityscapes.
GREEN ↔ MAGENTA
Natural/earthy feel. Green shadows with magenta highlights. Good for nature and park shots.

Rule of thumb: If you push shadows toward one complementary color, push highlights toward the other. You don't have to — monochromatic grades (shadows and highlights the same color, just different intensity) also work — but the complementary split is the most reliable starting point.

Color Temperature as a Storytelling Tool

Temperature (Warm/Cool) is the most emotionally powerful axis in color. Warm photos feel welcoming, safe, nostalgic, alive. Cool photos feel distant, cinematic, lonely, or dramatic. Neither is better — they tell different stories.

For your cats: Warm tones make the photos feel intimate and cozy — a cat at home, safe and comfortable. Cool tones would make the same cat feel more mysterious or dramatic. Your instinct to shoot warm is the right call for the emotional story you're telling.

For your architecture: Cleveland's industrial character often suits cooler, more desaturated grades. The city has a steel-grey quality that feels authentic at lower temperatures. You can contrast this by finding warm pockets of light within that cool structure — which is exactly what makes split toning so effective for urban photography.

The Global Color Wheel — When to Use It

The fourth wheel in Color Grading (the one you see when you click the single-circle icon, or the larger wheel in the middle when all three are visible) is the Global wheel. It shifts color across all tonal ranges simultaneously — shadows, midtones, and highlights all move the same direction.

When to use the Global wheel: When you want a monochromatic overall color cast — the whole image slightly warmer, the whole image slightly cooler, the whole image slightly tinted. It's less surgical than the three individual wheels but faster for overall mood shifts.

Typical use: Start with Global to establish the base temperature mood. Then use the three individual wheels to differentiate — make shadows pull one way, highlights another. Think of Global as your first big stroke and the three wheels as your detail work.

Color Grading vs HSL — Which to Use When

Use HSL whenFixing specific colorsYou want to change one specific color in the image without affecting others. Sky too bright? Lower Blue Luminance in HSL. Skin too orange? Reduce Orange Saturation in HSL. Grass looks yellow instead of green? Shift Yellow Hue in HSL toward green. HSL is surgical and color-specific.
Use Color Grading whenCreative mood / styleYou want to intentionally shift the feel of the entire image by range (shadows vs highlights). Color Grading doesn't target specific colors — it targets tonal ranges regardless of hue. It's your creative storytelling tool.
Use both togetherComplete color pipelineThe typical workflow: WB correction → HSL for fixing specific color issues → Color Grading for creative mood. They layer on top of each other and work together.
Vibrance/SaturationLast, after all color workGlobal saturation changes should come after you've done your color grading. Boosting saturation before grading amplifies your grade unpredictably.

Tone Curve for Color — The Channel Curves

In Lightroom's Tone Curve panel, click on the Point Curve button (small square icon) and look for a dropdown that says "RGB." Change it to Red, Green, or Blue to edit individual color channels as curves.

What this lets you do: Pulling the Blue channel's shadows down pushes shadows toward yellow/orange (removing blue from shadows). Pulling the Blue channel's highlights up pushes highlights toward blue. This is an older method of split toning that predates the Color Grading wheels — some editors prefer it for finer control.

Quick film-style trick with channel curves: In the Blue channel, pull the shadow endpoint up slightly (from 0 to about 15) — this lifts the blacks with a blue cast, creating a faded/film-print look. Very popular for moody urban shots. Pair with reduced Blacks in the main curve.

Color Grading Practice — Build Intuition Fast

1
The neutral sweep

Open any photo. Drag the Shadows wheel slowly all the way around the color circle, watching the image change. Then reset to center. Do the same for Midtones, then Highlights. This is how you learn what each wheel actually does to your specific photo. 10 minutes of this builds more intuition than reading for an hour.

2
Copy a grade from a photo you admire

Find a photo with a color grade you like — a movie still, a photographer's work, anything. Try to reverse-engineer it using the wheels. What color are the shadows? What color are the highlights? Try to match it on one of your own photos. You'll probably fail the first time. That failure teaches you more than success.

3
Apply one recipe to 5 very different photos

Take the Cleveland Urban recipe from the Looks tab. Apply it to an architecture shot, a cat shot, a nature shot, a motion shot, and a low-light shot. Notice where it works naturally and where you have to fight it. This teaches you which looks are versatile vs subject-specific.

4
Before/after toggle habit

Use the backslash key (\) in Lightroom to toggle before/after. After every color grading session, toggle it 5 times slowly. Ask: does the grade serve the photo or is it just "doing something?" If you can't articulate why the grade improves it, it probably doesn't. Reset and try again with more intention.

5
The 20% rule

Whatever you think looks good, reduce every color grading adjustment by 20%. New color graders almost always over-grade because the effect looks exciting on screen. But viewed later on a phone or printed, over-graded images look garish. Your first instinct for "how much" is almost always too much.

Masking — Selective Editing
Every mask type explained · How to build masks · Combining masks · Real examples for your subjects
What masking actually is: A mask is a way of telling Lightroom "apply this adjustment only to this specific part of the image." Without masks, every adjustment affects the whole photo. With masks, you can brighten just the subject's face, darken just the sky, sharpen just the cat's eyes, or add warmth only to the building and not the background. Masking is the difference between basic editing and professional editing.

How to Open the Masking Panel

In Lightroom's Develop module, click the Masking icon in the toolbar below the histogram (it looks like a circle cut in half), or press Shift+W. A panel opens showing all available mask types. Click any type to start creating that mask. Once created, a mask appears in the mask list and you set your adjustments in the panel below.

⬛
Linear Gradient
Sky darkening · Foreground lifting · Edge vignettes
Creates a straight-line gradient across the frame. The adjustment fades smoothly from full strength to nothing. Drag from the area you want most affected toward the area you want least affected.

Drag from top to bottom to darken just the sky. Drag from bottom to top to lift just the foreground. Drag from the side to affect one side of a building.
→ Best first mask to learn. Use it on every architectural shot with sky.
⭕
Radial Gradient
Subject spotlight · Eye enhancement · Vignette control
Creates an oval/elliptical gradient. By default, the adjustment applies outside the oval (like a vignette). Click "Invert" to apply it inside the oval instead (to spotlight your subject).

Draw an oval around Lincoln's face → Invert → boost Exposure +0.3, Shadows +20, Texture +15. The cat's face brightens while the background stays put. The feathered edge makes it invisible.
→ Most useful mask for subjects. Every cat portrait benefits from one around the face.
🖌️
Brush
Precise area edits · Sky, ground, object-specific
Paint exactly where you want the adjustment with a brush. Control Size (scroll wheel or [ ] keys), Feather (how soft the edges are), Flow (how strong each stroke is), and Density (maximum strength cap).

Paint only the typewriter's keys → add Clarity +25, Texture +20. Paint only a shadow under a building → add Shadows +30. The brush gives you pixel-level control.
→ Most flexible mask. Slow to use but most precise. Use for anything the other masks can't do cleanly.
🎨

Select sky by color · Isolate skin tones · Target specific hues

Click the blue sky → the mask selects all the sky automatically → reduce Exposure -0.5 and add Blue Luminance -20 in that mask → the sky darkens without touching the building at all.
→ Excellent for skies and any scene with distinct color separation between subject and background.
💡
Luminance Range
Select shadows only · Protect highlights · Noise reduction in shadows only
Creates a mask based on how bright or dark areas are. You set a luminance range with two sliders — the mask selects only pixels within that brightness range.

Set a dark luminance range → apply noise reduction only to the shadows (where it's needed) without softening the sharp bright areas. Or select only the highlights to add a slight warm tint to bright areas without affecting shadows.
→ Most technical mask. Very powerful for targeted noise reduction on FZ20 low-light shots.
🌊

Less relevant for FZ20 JPEG files, which usually don't contain depth map data. You can ignore this mask type for now.

Detects and selects the sky automatically, even through tree branches and complex edges. One click.

Workflow for architecture vs sky: Select Sky → reduce Exposure -0.7, add Highlights -40, shift Temp slightly cooler. This darkens and deepens the sky without touching the building at all. What used to require a careful linear gradient mask is now a single click.
→ Use on every outdoor architectural and landscape shot. Near-perfect selection every time.
🌿
Select Background
Everything except the subject
The inverse of Select Subject — selects everything in the background while leaving the main subject unmasked.

Use case: For typewriter shots where the desk background is distracting — Select Background → reduce Exposure -0.3, reduce Saturation -15, add slight blur via Texture -10. The typewriter pops forward while the background recedes without any manual masking.
→ Use when you want to modify the background without touching the subject.
👁️
People Masks (Face, Body, etc.)
Portrait-specific sub-selections
When a person is detected, Lightroom can create sub-masks for specific body parts: Face Skin, Body Skin, Eyes, Lips, Teeth, Eyebrows, Hair. Each is selectable separately.

Practical use: Select Eyes → boost Clarity +25, Exposure +0.2, Sharpness +15. Select Skin → add slight Smoothing (negative Texture -10) without affecting the eyes you just sharpened. Professional portrait retouching in under 2 minutes.
→ Available when Lightroom detects a human subject. Doesn't work for cats (yet).
AI masks aren't perfect. Select Subject occasionally includes bits of background or misses parts of the subject — especially with complex edges like cat fur or tree branches against sky. Always check the mask by pressing O in Lightroom to show the colored overlay — red areas are masked. Use the Brush tool (Add or Subtract mode) to clean up any imperfect edges.

Combining Masks — Add, Subtract, Intersect

Masks can be combined with each other to create complex selections that no single mask type could achieve alone. This is where masking becomes genuinely powerful.

The Three Operations

ADD (+)
Expands the mask to include more area. If Select Sky missed a patch of sky behind a tree branch, Add a Brush stroke over that patch to include it in the existing mask. Use the "Add" button that appears when you click an existing mask.
SUBTRACT (−)
Removes areas from the mask. If Select Subject accidentally included part of the background, Subtract a Brush stroke over that area to remove it from the selection. Critical for cleaning up imperfect AI masks.
INTERSECT (∩)
Keeps only the overlap of two masks. Intersect is the most powerful combination. Example: Create a Select Subject mask (selects the cat) → Intersect with a Luminance Range set to dark tones → the resulting mask selects only the dark areas within the cat. You can then boost just the shadow detail in the fur without brightening the already-bright areas of the cat.

Practical Combination Examples

Sky + not buildingSelect Sky → Subtract brush on building edgesWhen the sky mask bleeds onto the top of a building, subtract a brush stroke along the roofline to clean the edge. Then darken the sky alone perfectly.
Cat fur detail onlySelect Subject → Intersect Luminance Range (dark)Selects only the dark shadow areas within the cat's silhouette. Lift Shadows +30 and add Texture +20 to reveal fur detail in the darkest areas without brightening the cat's face or already-visible fur.
Bright sky + keep sunSelect Sky → Subtract Luminance Range (very bright)Select sky to darken it, but subtract a luminance range mask of the very brightest areas to protect the sun itself from going dark. The sky darkens beautifully while the sun stays bright.
Architecture shadows onlySelect Subject (building) → Intersect Luminance Range (dark)Selects only the shadowed areas of the building. Lift Shadows +35 to reveal brick/stone detail in shadow. The sunlit face of the building is protected from overexposure.
Typewriter + no keysSelect Subject → Subtract Color Range (key color)Select the whole typewriter, then subtract the specific color of the keys to edit only the metal body without changing the key tops. Allows independent editing of each element.

Complete Masking Workflow — Step by Step

1
Always finish global edits first

Complete all your Light panel (exposure, contrast, shadows, highlights), Color/WB, and Color Grading work before opening the Masking panel. Masks are the final refinement layer — if you change global settings after creating masks, your masks may need to be re-evaluated.

2
Open Masking panel (Shift+W)

Click "Create New Mask." Choose your mask type based on what you're trying to isolate. For most shots, start with Select Subject or Select Sky — the AI tools are accurate and fast. For more complex selections, use Linear/Radial Gradient or Brush.

3
Check the mask overlay (press O)

Press O to toggle the red overlay showing exactly what's masked. Zoom into edges. Check for areas that should be included but aren't (or shouldn't be included but are). Use Add/Subtract brush to clean up imperfect edges.

4
Apply your adjustment to the mask

With the mask selected in the list, the adjustment panel below becomes active. Only adjust what you actually need — don't touch every slider. Typical mask adjustments are 1–3 sliders maximum. More than that usually means you need a different approach.

5
Duplicate and invert for the opposite

Right-click any mask in the list → "Duplicate Mask" → then click the three dots on the duplicate → "Invert Mask." Now you have two masks covering opposite areas of the frame. Apply complementary adjustments — what you did to the sky, reverse the direction for the ground. Fast way to balance a photo.

6
Keep your mask list organized

Double-click any mask in the list to rename it ("Sky Darken," "Cat Face Lift," "Building Shadow Detail"). When you have 4–6 masks on one photo, named masks are the only way to know what you're editing without toggling each one on and off.

Common masking mistakes: (1) Masking before global edits are done — change your exposure after and your masks are fighting your global work. (2) Using too many masks — if you need 8+ masks, you probably over-corrected something globally and are now compensating everywhere. Fix the global edit first. (3) Over-feathering brushes — a brush with 100% feather creates invisible transitions but also means the adjustment slowly fades and never fully applies anywhere. Use 60–80% feather for most work.
